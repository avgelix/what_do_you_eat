This is what breakfast often looks like in Italy, where I grew up.

Milk and cereal in a bowl, (soy milk for my lactose-intolerant friends).

My breakfast habits did not change much since moving to the USA in 2021. And while there's many other ways to have braekfast, I usualy seek the milk+cereal combo where I happen to be (and I always find it!)

I did not ask myself too many questions about the different milks and cerelas I was consuming, until my body started demanding an answer I am still desperately looking for: What do you eat?

That same breakfast looks equally inviting regardless of where in the world I am, but it is not nutritionally equivalent. As global citizens, eating consciously means understanding the invisible differences that make breakfast in Italy different from breakfast in Japan, even when the food in your plate looks nearly identical.

Everyday foods

What are foods you eat regularly? Milk, bread, yogurt, pasta... These are some of the most common food products worldwide.

This project analyzes how the compositions of these common foods differ across five countries from different continents.

If that gave you a taste of what the invisible differences between food product are, what's next will paint a fuller picture.

I identified 50 staple foods and gathered data for each of them to understand how their composition varies all around the world. In order to do this, I looked for products representing a food staple in one country from each continent.

How complex are the foods we eat?

Not all food categories are equal. The data shows some are highly complex across all countries, like energy drinks, while others stay simple overall, like whole milk. However, some food categories vary significantly between countries, most notably infant formula, ham, bread products, and cheeses.

What This Means For Conscious Eating

The data reveals three important patterns:

  1. Geographic differences are real: Products with identical labels contain vastly different ingredients depending on where they're sold.
  2. Processing levels vary: Some countries favor simpler formulations, while others embrace complexity.
  3. Transparency matters: Understanding what we eat requires accessible, open data.

Health changes aren't coincidental. When the "same" foods are fundamentally different at the ingredient level we need to listen to our bodies and develop awareness about what we consume.
